What is Economics?

  • “Economics is the study of mankind in the ordinary business of life” – Alfred Marshal

  • “Economics deals with all human action” – Ludwig von Mises

  • Economics is the study of how society manages its scarce resources” – Gregory Mankiw

  • “People respond to incentives. Everything else is commentary” – Steve Landsburg

  • “The curious task of economics is to demonstrate to men how little they really know about what they imagine they can design.” – FA Hayek

Micro & Macro

  • Microeconomics focuses on the actions of individual agents within the economy, like households, workers, and businesses

  • Macroeconomics is the branch of economics that focuses on broad issues such as growth, unemployment, inflation, and trade balance

Economics

  • Economics: the study of the efficient allocation of scarce goods
  • Scarcity: the conflict of limited resources with unlimited desires
    • Resources (land, labor, capital and entrepreneurship) and time

Scarcity requires choice

  • Scarcity requires choice in allocation

  • Three fundamental allocation questions:

    • What to produce?
    • How to produce it?
    • For whom to produce?

Wants vs. Needs

  • Needs are relatively objective (e.g. daily nutritional requirements)
  • Wants are more subjective and context-dependent (e.g. air conditioning)
  • We may eliminate poverty (objective), but we will never eliminate scarcity

Scarcity is not the same as poverty

Scarcity is not the same as poverty

Rationing

  • One uncomfortable fact: scarcity requires rationing
    • When something is scarce, there must be some criteria for determining who will receive it (and who will not)
  • Changing how goods are allocated only changes the form of competition, it cannot not eliminate it

Rationing

  • Government rationing

    • favors those with better political connections

  • First-come, first-serve

  • favors those who get in line first

  • Markets

    • favors those who are willing and able to pay higher prices

Scarcity Creates Opportunity Costs

  • The use of resources to produce one good diverts those resources from the production of other goods
    • A decision to pursue any option means that another option must be forgone
    • “There are no solutions, only tradeoffs”
  • Opportunity cost: the highest valued alternative that is sacrificed whenever a decision is made

Scarcity Creates Opportunity Costs

Individuals chose purposefully

  • Economists assume that individuals will choose the options that best advance their personal desires and goals at the least possible cost (a.k.a. “economizing behavior”)
    • This implies that individual preferences are "revealed" by their choices

Incentives Matter

  • Changes in incentives affect human behavior in predictable ways
    • Free shipping at $35 → carts bunch at $35
    • Surge pricing → more willing drivers
    • Toll/express lanes → drivers with low time value change behavior
    • Rent control → fewer new units, reduced maintenance
    • Decrease term limits → more public debt

Incentives Matter

Incentives Matter

  • “In the former Soviet Union, managers and employees of glass plants were at one time rewarded according to the tons of sheet glass they produced. Because their revenues depended on the weight of the glass, most factories produced sheet glass so thick that you could hardly see through it…”

  • “…The rules were changed so that the managers were compensated according to the number of square meters of glass they could produce.”

  • “…Under these rules, Soviet firms made glass so thin that it broke easily.”

Decisions should be made at the margin

    • Tuition: $2,244 (12 hours) - $2,805 (15+ hours); $187.00 per credit hour
    • Fees: $800

    • Marginal cost of class = Opportunity Cost

Decisions should be made at the margin

  • The most famous example of failing to think at the margin is known as the sunk cost fallacy
    • Informing future decisions on past costs is irrational and inefficient
  • Examples:
    • “I can’t move now; my lease isn’t over for another 5 months”
    • “I spent 20 hours studying for the midterm, I can’t drop the class now”
    • “The ticket was expensive, so I’m going even though I’m sick”

Sunk Cost Fallacy

  • Examples
    • “We’ve been together since 10th grade; we can’t break up”
    • “I’ve watched 6 seasons; I have to finish the series even if I hate it”
    • “I’ve already put $2k into repairs; I shouldn’t sell the car now”
    • “I’ve waited 45 minutes on hold; I can’t hang up now”
    • “Hold the losing stock until I ‘get back to even’”
    • “The city has spent so much on this project; canceling would ‘waste’ the money”

Sunk Cost Fallacy

Beware of secondary costs

  • Plastic bags are a large environmental cost given that they take ~20 years to decompose
    • Emergency room visits and deaths from E. coli rose by 25% following San Francisco’s plastic bag ban
  • People systematically underestimate their driving abilities. Thus, the government requires individuals purchase insurance
    • Accidents are less expensive, and thus people drive more dangerously
  • Oil spills have massive environmental costs. Thus, some governments uncapped oil company liability
    • Companies hire (less experienced) contractors to minimize liability costs → increasing spills
